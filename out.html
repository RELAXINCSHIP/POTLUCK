1. Product Overview

1.1 What Potluck Is

Potluck is a non-custodial, DeFi-native prize-linked savings application built on Abstract L2 (an Ethereum Layer 2 network). Users deposit USDC stablecoin into an audited ERC-4626 smart contract vault. That vault deploys deposited capital to Aave (a decentralized lending protocol) to generate yield. The yield — not the principal — is periodically swept into a prize pool. A provably fair draw using Chainlink VRF (Verifiable Random Function) selects a winning ticket holder every 90 days. The winner receives the accumulated prize pool. All other depositors retain 100% of their original principal and may withdraw at any time.

1.2 What Potluck Is Not

  • We are not a bank, credit union, or depository institution
  • We do not custody user funds — funds are held in a non-custodial smart contract
  • We do not operate a lottery — no tickets are purchased, no money is spent to enter
  • We do not guarantee any return — yield is variable and market-dependent
  • We do not sell securities — users are not investing in a common enterprise managed by Potluck

1.3 The Core Legal Thesis We Are Operating Under

Potluck is structured on the legal theory that a non-custodial smart contract protocol, where users interact directly with on-chain contracts and Potluck never takes custody of funds, falls outside the definition of money transmission under most state MSB statutes. We are seeking counsel to confirm, challenge, or refine this thesis before launch.

KEY STRUCTURAL CHOICE

We deliberately chose Abstract L2 + ERC-4626 + Chainlink VRF specifically to minimize regulatory surface area. There is no bank partner. There is no BaaS intermediary. There is no Synapse. User funds are in the smart contract — not in an account held by Potluck or a partner institution. This is the opposite of how Yotta and PrizePool.com were structured, both of which collapsed when their banking partners failed.

2. Technical & Legal Structure

2.1 Smart Contract Architecture

The protocol consists of three smart contracts, all deployed on Abstract L2:

PotluckVault.sol

ERC-4626 compliant tokenized vault. Accepts USDC deposits. Routes capital to Aave lending pools to generate yield. Issues non-transferable vault shares to depositors. Principal always redeemable directly from contract.

PotluckTickets.sol

Non-transferable ERC-1155 ticket contract. Issues draw entries proportional to deposit size and time-weighted streak multiplier. Cannot be bought, sold, or transferred — entries are earned only by depositing.

PrizePool.sol

Accumulates yield swept from PotluckVault. Calls Chainlink VRF to request verifiable randomness. Resolves winning ticket. Distributes prize to winner wallet automatically and immediately on-chain.

2.3 Custody Analysis

At no point does Potluck, Inc. (or its principals) take possession, custody, or control of user funds. Users interact directly with the smart contract. Potluck deploys and maintains the smart contract code but cannot access, freeze, move, or confiscate user funds. Even in a scenario where Potluck ceases operations entirely, users retain the ability to call withdraw() on the smart contract directly.

CUSTODY POSITION

Potluck's position is that it is a protocol developer, not a custodian or money transmitter. This is analogous to the position taken by Uniswap Labs, Aave Companies, and other DeFi protocol developers operating in the United States. We seek counsel's opinion on whether this characterization holds in our specific fact pattern.

3. Specific Legal Questions for Counsel

We are asking counsel to provide written opinion on each of the following questions. Where the answer is jurisdiction-dependent, we are initially focused on launch in the United States with particular attention to California, New York, Texas, and Florida as our anticipated highest-volume states.

Question 1 — Money Transmission

THE QUESTION

Does Potluck's operation constitute money transmission under (a) the federal Bank Secrecy Act / FinCEN MSB regulations, and (b) applicable state money transmitter statutes, given that Potluck never takes custody of user funds and users interact directly with non-custodial smart contracts?

RELEVANT FACTS

  • Potluck never receives, holds, or transmits user funds
  • Users transfer USDC directly to PotluckVault.sol via their own wallet
  • Potluck cannot access, move, or freeze funds in the contract
  • Yield is moved between smart contracts by automated contract logic, not by Potluck
  • Prize distribution is executed autonomously by PrizePool.sol, not by Potluck

CONTEXT & PRECEDENT

FinCEN's 2019 guidance on convertible virtual currency (FIN-2019-G001) provides that a person who provides anonymizing services or operates a decentralized exchange may be an MSB. However, the guidance also notes that miners and validators who do not engage in transmission are not MSBs. We believe our fact pattern is closer to the latter — we are protocol developers, not transmitters. We seek counsel's view on whether this characterization is defensible.

Question 2 — Prize-Linked Savings Regulation

THE QUESTION

Does Potluck's prize pool mechanic constitute an illegal lottery or raffle under federal or state law, or does it qualify for protection under prize-linked savings exemptions? Specifically: does the American Savings Promotion Act of 2014 (12 U.S.C. § 1832(e)) apply to or protect Potluck's structure, and if not, what is our exposure under state lottery laws?

RELEVANT FACTS

  • No consideration is paid to enter — users do not purchase tickets or pay fees to participate in the draw
  • Draw entries are earned by depositing into a savings-equivalent vehicle (USDC vault)
  • Principal is never at risk — all deposited funds are fully redeemable at any time
  • The prize pool is funded solely by yield generated on deposits, not by user entry fees
  • The draw mechanism is provably fair and publicly verifiable via Chainlink VRF

CONTEXT & PRECEDENT

The American Savings Promotion Act explicitly permits FDIC-insured depository institutions to offer prize-linked savings accounts. Credit unions in Michigan, Nebraska, Washington, and 40+ other states operate similar prize-linked savings products under state-level statutory exemptions. Potluck is not an FDIC-insured institution. We seek counsel's view on whether our structure falls within, outside, or adjacent to these frameworks, and what our exposure is under the lottery laws of our target states.

Question 3 — Securities Classification

THE QUESTION

Does Potluck's vault share or prize pool mechanic constitute a security under the Securities Act of 1933, the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, or the Howey test? Specifically: do vault shares (issued by PotluckVault.sol) or draw entries (issued by PotluckTickets.sol) constitute investment contracts?

THE HOWEY ANALYSIS WE HAVE PERFORMED

The Howey test requires: (1) an investment of money, (2) in a common enterprise, (3) with an expectation of profits, (4) derived from the efforts of others. We believe our structure challenges prongs 3 and 4 as follows:

  • Prong 3 (expectation of profits) — Users deposit to save, not to profit. Principal is always returned. The prize is not a guaranteed return — it is probabilistic and most users will not receive it. Users have no expectation of profit in the securities law sense.
  • Prong 4 (efforts of others) — Yield is generated by Aave, a separate protocol Potluck does not control. The draw is executed by Chainlink VRF, a separate oracle Potluck does not control. Potluck's ongoing involvement is maintenance of the frontend and smart contracts — not the management of an investment.

QUESTION FOR COUNSEL

We seek counsel's view on whether this analysis is defensible, and whether either vault shares or draw entries could be characterized as securities by the SEC under current enforcement posture or the Gensler-era interpretive positions.

Question 4 — Card Issuance (Phase 2)

THE QUESTION

What regulatory pathway is required to issue a branded physical payment card ("The Meridian") to Potluck users? Specifically: what BIN sponsorship, card network (Visa/Mastercard) membership, and state licensing requirements apply? What is a realistic timeline and cost estimate for achieving card issuance capability?

CONTEXT

The Meridian card is a Phase 2 product — it is not part of the launch. We are building toward it and need to understand the regulatory pathway now so we can structure Phase 2 correctly. We are aware that card issuance requires a bank issuing partner or BIN sponsor (e.g., Marqeta, Lithic, or a direct bank partner). We seek counsel's view on the fastest and cleanest path to card issuance given our DeFi-native structure.

Question 5 — USDC / Stablecoin Considerations

THE QUESTION

Are there any regulatory or contractual constraints from Circle (USDC issuer), the MiCA framework (for future EU expansion), or proposed US stablecoin legislation that would affect Potluck's use of USDC as its primary deposit currency? Are there acceptable use policy considerations we should be aware of before launching at scale?

Question 6 — Corporate Structure

THE QUESTION

We intend to incorporate as a Delaware C-Corp for the seed round. Should the protocol itself be operated by a separate foundation or DAO structure for regulatory insulation? Are there jurisdictional advantages to incorporating the operating company outside the US (e.g., Cayman Islands, BVI, Singapore) while maintaining US operations?

5. Comparable Structures for Reference

The following existing structures may be instructive for counsel's analysis:

PoolTogether

Non-custodial prize savings protocol on Ethereum. Operated in the US for years before receiving a cease-and-desist from Kentucky. Subsequently restructured as a DAO. Their regulatory history is the most directly relevant precedent for Potluck.

Uniswap Labs

Protocol developer that does not custody funds. Has operated in the US while maintaining the position that it is a software developer, not a financial intermediary. SEC Wells notice received 2024 — outcome pending.

Aave Companies

Protocol developer for the Aave lending protocol (which Potluck's vault routes to). Has operated in the US without MTL. CFTC and SEC have not taken action against Aave Companies as of this writing.

Credit Union PLSAs

Michigan, Nebraska, Washington, and 40+ other states have credit unions offering prize-linked savings under state statutory exemptions. The Save to Win program (Michigan) is the longest-running example. These are the closest traditional finance analogues.

Yotta / PrizePool.com

Both operated prize-linked savings via BaaS (bank-as-a-service) partners and both collapsed when their banking partners failed. These are the cautionary precedents. Potluck's non-custodial structure is explicitly designed to avoid this failure mode.

NS&I Premium Bonds (UK)

Operated by NS&I (UK government-backed) since 1956. The world’s largest prize-linked savings product: £122 billion in bonds outstanding, 22 million holders (1 in 3 British adults). Holders deposit money, earn no interest, and instead receive monthly prize draw entries. Prizes range from £25 to £1,000,000. Principal 100% guaranteed by HM Treasury. Draw conducted by ERNIE (Electronic Random Number Indicator Equipment) — government’s equivalent of Chainlink VRF. The UK government has ruled for 70 years that this structure is NOT gambling because no consideration is lost. This is the strongest available international precedent for the argument that prize-linked savings is a distinct legal category from lottery or gambling. Potluck is the on-chain, DeFi-native equivalent — open to all, with transparent yield rates and publicly verifiable draws.
